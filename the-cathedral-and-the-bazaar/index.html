2. Cathedral and Bazaar

  1. Good software works often come from the personal needs of the developers
  2. A good programmer knows what to write, a good programmer knows what to rewrite (and reuse)
  3. ‚ÄúPlan to throw one away. Sooner or later you will do it.‚Äù (Fred Brooks, Chapter 11 of The Myth of Man-Month)
  4. If you have the right attitude, interesting things will naturally find you.
  5. When you are no longer interested in a program, your last responsibility is to give it to a competent successor
  6. Treat your users as development collaborators. This is the most worry-free way if you want to quickly improve code quality and effectively troubleshoot errors.
  7. Publish early, publish often, and listen to user feedback.
  8. If there are enough beta testers and co-developers, almost all problems will appear quickly, and then naturally someone will solve it.
  • Linus‚Äôs law tells the most critical difference between the cathedral mode and the market mode: in the eyes of the cathedral builders, bugs are tricky, hard to find, and hidden in the depths. It takes several months of dedication. With careful inspection, you can be confident that all errors have been eliminated. And the longer the release interval, if the long-awaited release version is not perfect, people‚Äôs disappointment becomes more inevitable.
  • For the market model, it is completely different. With thousands of co-developers eagerly studying each new release, you can assume that bugs are easy to find, or at least quickly become easy to find. So you will release frequently to get more corrections, and the side effect is benign: even if there are some minor issues in the release, you will not lose too much.
  1. Smart data structures with stupid code are far better than the other way around.
  2. If you treat beta testers as your most precious resource, they will become your most precious resource
  3. Next to having a good idea is to identify good ideas from users, sometimes the latter is better
  4. Usually, the most breakthrough and innovative solutions come from the realization that your basic idea of the problem is wrong.
  5. ‚ÄúPerfection in design is not that nothing can be added, but that nothing can be subtracted.‚Äù
  6. Any tool should have expected functions, but a great tool can bring you unexpected functions.
  7. When writing gateway software, try not to interfere with the data flow as much as possible, and never throw away the information, unless the recipient forces you to do so
  8. When your language is far from Turing-complete, syntactic sugar will benefit you a lot.
  9. The security of the system depends only on the secrets it possesses. Beware of false secrets.
  10. To solve an interesting problem, first find a problem that interests you.
  11. If the development coordinator has a good communication medium like the Internet at least, and knows how to lead without coercion, then multi-person cooperation must be better than individual combat
  • If you feel fear and disgust during work (even if you express it in the form of self-deprecating-such as hanging a Dilbert doll), you should realize that something went wrong with the process. Happiness, humor and playfulness are real assets
  • ‚ÄúPlay‚Äù is the most economically effective work mode in creative activities.

3. Cultivate the mental layer

  • In gift culture, social status does not depend on what you control, but what you give
  1. If it doesn‚Äôt work as I expected, it‚Äôs not good-no matter how smart and original it is.
  2. Extensive work at the mental level is better than repetitive work in a functional domain (for existing works).
  3. Works that can enter the main release are better than those that cannot. The works included in all major releases are the most respectable.
  4. ‚ÄúUse‚Äù is the most true compliment, and the category killer is better than its competitors.

  1. Reliability/stability/scalability is very important.
  2. Except for independent peer review, there is no other convenient and easy way to verify the correctness of the design and implementation.
  3. The software is very critical to the customer‚Äôs business.
  4. The software creates or operates a public computing or communication infrastructure.
  5. Key methods (or methods that can achieve equivalent functions) are public knowledge

Appendix A: How to become a hacker

Hackers do construction, hackers do damage

Hacker attitude

  1. The world is full of fascinating problems waiting for people to solve.
  2. Don‚Äôt solve a problem twice.
  3. Boredom and boredom are harmful
  4. Freedom is good
  5. Attitude cannot replace ability.

Basic skills of hackers

  1. Learn to program
  • If you want to acquire a mindset that transforms knowledge into skills, you can only practice and follow the master. The way is to read code and write code
  1. Find an open source UNIX, learn to use and run it.
  2. Learn how to use WWW and write HTML.

Status in hacker culture

  1. Write open source software.
  2. Assist in testing and debugging open source software
  3. Publish useful information.
  4. Help do some basic work.
  5. Serve the hacker culture itself.

Style problem

  • Learn to write well in your native language. Although there is an old belief that programmers can‚Äôt write well, many hackers (including the best hackers I know) write good articles, and the number is surprisingly large.

  • Read science fiction and participate in science fiction rally (this is a good way to get to know hackers and potential hackers).

  • Study meditation and/or learn martial arts. (Mental training seems to be similar in many important ways.)

  • Cultivate the appreciation of music. Learn to appreciate some unique types of music. Learn to play an instrument well, or learn how to sing.

  • Cultivate your appreciation of puns and witty words.

    But these things are related to the combination of left and right brain skills, which may be the key to the problem (hackers not only need to have strong logical reasoning skills, but also be able to quickly escape from the specific logic of the problem)

Don‚Äôt

  • Don‚Äôt use stupid, exaggerated usernames or nicknames.
  • Don‚Äôt get involved in Usenet (or anywhere else) online scolding wars.
  • Don‚Äôt call yourself ‚Äúcyberpunk‚Äù, and don‚Äôt waste time interacting with people who call themselves cyberpunk.
  • Don‚Äôt let your posts or emails be full of incorrect spelling and bad grammar.

At last:
‚ÄúYou are a hacker only if other hackers think you are a hacker‚Äù
The values expressed are similar to the ideals declared by the elite groups of the medieval European chivalry era (usually unachievable). These social elites have sufficient financial resources to surpass the scarce economy in which they are located. As one might expect, an aspiring knight fights for justice. He seeks honor instead of money. He stands on the side of the weak and oppressed, constantly seeking opportunities to challenge the limits of his heroic talents. It is precisely because of this that he will consider himself (and considered by others) to be a leader among people-provided that his abilities and conduct have been recognized and officially recognized by other knights. In the chivalry eulogized by Arthurian tales and chansons de geste, we see idealism, constant challenge to oneself and the pursuit of status, which are similar to the elements that motivate hackers today.
